Salon #18 - 10/7/15

Zoe read a hilarious and insightful essay from the Paris Review about Robert Frost's often misinterpreted poem, "The Road Not Taken". The piece deconstructs the outsize popularity of the poem, as well as its meaning. Here is a representative quote:

"Most readers consider “The Road Not Taken” to be a paean to triumphant self-assertion (“I took the one less traveled by”), but the literal meaning of the poem’s own lines seems completely at odds with this interpretation. The poem’s speaker tells us he “shall be telling,” at some point in the future, of how he took the road less traveled by, yet he has already admitted that the two paths “equally lay / In leaves” and “the passing there / Had worn them really about the same.” So the road he will later call less traveled is actually the road equally traveled. The two roads are interchangeable. According to this reading, then, the speaker will be claiming “ages and ages hence” that his decision made “all the difference” only because this is the kind of claim we make when we want to comfort or blame ourselves by assuming that our current position is the product of our own choices (as opposed to what was chosen for us or allotted to us by chance). The poem isn’t a salute to can-do individualism; it’s a commentary on the self-deception we practice when constructing the story of our own lives."

Salon #21 - 11/18/15

Another great, big group! Zoe brought and read Arthur Miller's Tragedy and the Common Man, and we discussed tragedy, both in the academic/ literary sense, and as a way of processing the current violence and tragedy that is happening globally. In particular, we discussed if/ how the literary definition of tragedy (an exalted figure who is brought down by a tragic flaw) can be reconciled with our more colloquial understanding of tragedy. Then, Shirlee gave a fantastic introduction to and explanation of CRISPR-cas9, a new gene editing technology which is taking the world by storm. This is a potentially revolutionally new technology that could have major implications for healthcare, ethics, and beyond. Researchers found that a certain bacterial gene locus which had a pattern of repeating segments (e.g., the CRISPRs) interspersed with other sequences of bases (say, "NNNN") could match with, and cut, that pattern "NNNN" in another gene. This bacteria brings in the cas9 proteins which are able to do the actual cutting. Then, the CRISPR sequence, with interspersed patterns provides a template for the gene to repair itself against. This is how a gene can be edited - both additively and subtractively! The CRISPR sequences and interspersed patterns are synthesized; the patterns are synthesized to match certain sequences in actual DNA. Shirlee gave a great presentation that made the information accessible on multiple levels!

To kick off the evening, I (Caroline J) read Frederic Laloux's essay: The Future of Management Is Teal. I discovered Laloux by reading his book, Reinventing Organizations, in one of my classes. After this discussion, Zoe gave a fantastic presentation on the quirks and uncertainties of Elizabethan publishing practices, especially regarding Shakespeare's oeuvre and popular misconceptions about its consistency. We learned that the Elizabethan era held very different notions of ownership and authorship. Because anybody could transcribe a play and sell it to a publisher, and because the publishers often (inadvertently) inserted typos or inconsistencies into the quartos and folios they sold, there exist many versions of Shakespeare's plays. This understanding casts new light on the modern practice of close textual analysis; Zoe gave the example of how uncertainty about a single letter in a single word near the end of Othello could lead to radically different interpretations of the play.

O'Neil discusses how algorithms impact everything from credit scores to wellness assessments and policing tactics. Salon #43 - 2/23/17

We enjoyed a small but cozy salon during one of our rare Thursday gatherings. I read Rebecca Solnit's funny yet scathing feminist essay, Men Explain Things to Me. This essay has been widely credited with giving rise to the term "mansplaining". We discussed different experiences in our careers and personal lives that we felt exemplified the ideas in the essay, and we discussed strategies for combatting mansplaining and sexism. I appreciated how outspoken and forthright Solnit is - many other authors seem to tiptoe around the ideas in the essay, but she is very forward in her assertion that the issue discussed in her essay is related to gender. After this discussion, Aviva read the first chapter of David Koeppel's fascinating book, Banana, which discusses the history of the modern banana. We learned about the banana's role and cultivation throughout history, from the Garden of Eden (some scholars contend that Eve ate a banana instead of an apple) to its outsize role in the "Banana Republics" of the early 20th Century, to the current struggle of food scientists to evade Panama Disease. The banana is the most-consumed fruit in the USA, and the fourth-most cultivated crop globally. Complex supply chains that manage how bananas ripen have been developed to bring a predictable, robust product to consumers all over the globe. Turns out there is a lot of intrigue behind the humble banana!
