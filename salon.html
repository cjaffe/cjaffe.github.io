Salon #18 - 10/7/15

Zoe read a hilarious and insightful essay from the Paris Review about Robert Frost's often misinterpreted poem, "The Road Not Taken". The piece deconstructs the outsize popularity of the poem, as well as its meaning. Here is a representative quote:

"Most readers consider “The Road Not Taken” to be a paean to triumphant self-assertion (“I took the one less traveled by”), but the literal meaning of the poem’s own lines seems completely at odds with this interpretation. The poem’s speaker tells us he “shall be telling,” at some point in the future, of how he took the road less traveled by, yet he has already admitted that the two paths “equally lay / In leaves” and “the passing there / Had worn them really about the same.” So the road he will later call less traveled is actually the road equally traveled. The two roads are interchangeable. According to this reading, then, the speaker will be claiming “ages and ages hence” that his decision made “all the difference” only because this is the kind of claim we make when we want to comfort or blame ourselves by assuming that our current position is the product of our own choices (as opposed to what was chosen for us or allotted to us by chance). The poem isn’t a salute to can-do individualism; it’s a commentary on the self-deception we practice when constructing the story of our own lives."

Salon #21 - 11/18/15

Another great, big group! Zoe brought and read Arthur Miller's Tragedy and the Common Man, and we discussed tragedy, both in the academic/ literary sense, and as a way of processing the current violence and tragedy that is happening globally. In particular, we discussed if/ how the literary definition of tragedy (an exalted figure who is brought down by a tragic flaw) can be reconciled with our more colloquial understanding of tragedy. Then, Shirlee gave a fantastic introduction to and explanation of CRISPR-cas9, a new gene editing technology which is taking the world by storm. This is a potentially revolutionally new technology that could have major implications for healthcare, ethics, and beyond. Researchers found that a certain bacterial gene locus which had a pattern of repeating segments (e.g., the CRISPRs) interspersed with other sequences of bases (say, "NNNN") could match with, and cut, that pattern "NNNN" in another gene. This bacteria brings in the cas9 proteins which are able to do the actual cutting. Then, the CRISPR sequence, with interspersed patterns provides a template for the gene to repair itself against. This is how a gene can be edited - both additively and subtractively! The CRISPR sequences and interspersed patterns are synthesized; the patterns are synthesized to match certain sequences in actual DNA. Shirlee gave a great presentation that made the information accessible on multiple levels!

To kick off the evening, I (Caroline J) read Frederic Laloux's essay: The Future of Management Is Teal. I discovered Laloux by reading his book, Reinventing Organizations, in one of my classes. After this discussion, Zoe gave a fantastic presentation on the quirks and uncertainties of Elizabethan publishing practices, especially regarding Shakespeare's oeuvre and popular misconceptions about its consistency. We learned that the Elizabethan era held very different notions of ownership and authorship. Because anybody could transcribe a play and sell it to a publisher, and because the publishers often (inadvertently) inserted typos or inconsistencies into the quartos and folios they sold, there exist many versions of Shakespeare's plays. This understanding casts new light on the modern practice of close textual analysis; Zoe gave the example of how uncertainty about a single letter in a single word near the end of Othello could lead to radically different interpretations of the play.

At salon, we spent 10-20 min discussing each initiative, as well as a few minutes discussing the race for Governor (Baker v. Gonzalez). For each race, we talked about the issue at hand, arguments for either side, and who was funding each side of each issue. Although there are no particularly controversial races in MA this year, it was useful to become informed on what we'll see on the ballot in a few weeks! Ballotpedia and Commonwealth Magazine were two useful resources for us. Also, Kat D. came over for a fun baking extravaganza beforehand; we made apple tart and squash bread for salon, both of which were delicious!


Salon #71 - 11/28/18

After a longer-than-usual hiatus from salon (Thanksgiving, travel, life, etc), we had a bigger-than-ever-before (15 people!) salon gathering with several new faces! It's always fun to welcome new people into the fold. We kicked things off with a reading from a new friend from MIT, who mentioned that her background of studying poetry often conflicts with the more scientific, technical language used at MIT. She brought readings from three different books to explore this idea: The Princeton Encyclopedia of Poetry and Poetics, The Triggering Town, and Beyond the Utmost Purple Rim. The first reading explored the history of the concepts of science and poetry, and talked about when these concepts diverged. The second reading spoke to the idea that you need to experience new things and places to understand the environment that you came from. The final reading was an example of a straightfoward story (nonfiction!) told in flowery, beautiful prose. Following the readings, we had a wide-ranging conversation about different types of language, changes in the way we describe our surroundsings, and the idea that bias can be built into even the most scientifc publication (e.g. a description of how eggs are fertilized!)

Afterwards, we were extremely lucky to hear a performance and presentation from salon member Rose Hegele, who is a highly accomplished vocalist. Rose performs what is variously called "new music", "contemporary music", or "unpopular music" (her term!). She gave two short vocal performances that were unlike anything I have ever heard before; composed of onomatopoeias and new sounds, the performances were alternatively melodic, theatrical, and bizarre. Rose also spent some time telling us about the origins of this type of music, how she learns new pieces and interprets them (the score for one of the pieces looked like a comic strip!), discussing the benefits of listening to live performances, and reflecting on the privilege of getting to engage in art on a daily basis. All in all, a huge treat to hear her performance and thoughtful reflections!


Salon Holiday Crafts Party - 12/12/18

To celebrate the end of the year and get a bit festive, we had a crafts and cookie decorating party! I made plain sugar cookies and Smitten Kitchen's matcha shortbread cookies (I love these!), along with royal icing that was way too runny! I also bought a gingerbread house kit from Whole Foods that people decorated with mixed results - fun concept but the icing was also too runny. In addition to these sweets, we made a few holiday crafts: felt Christmas trees (super cute), yarn-wrapped stars (fun + easy!), recycled paper stars, classic paper snowflakes, and glittery round ornaments. It was lovely to see so many people in a relaxed setting before the end of the year, and I appreciate everyone's creativity and contributions to making our house more festive! I also retrofitted the origami we had made in May as Christmas ornaments by adding an "ornament cap" at the top of each piece so that they could be hung up on the tree. I loved that all our Christmas ornaments were handmade this year!


Salon #72 - 2/6/19

It was lovely to convene a salon group after a longer-than-usual hiatus over IAP. After introductions, Snoweria read us excerpts from Ernest Renan's 1882 essay What is a Nation?. The essay explored national identities and how they are formed, variously discussing race, religion, geography, and shared history. It was interesting to consider Renan's essay in light of 2019 America. After the reading, Andrea and I shared some photos from our Colombia trip, and told about the communities we had worked with and the projects we developed there.
